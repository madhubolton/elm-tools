BC Old-Growth
Logging

When worlds with incompatible Four Rooms collide — a dynamical mapping of the oldest forest conflict in Canada
Standard analysis of the BC old-growth conflict frames it as a policy problem: how much timber, which areas protected, what consultation process, what transition funding. These are necessary but insufficient. They treat the conflict as a dispute within a single shared world — one in which the forest is agreed to be a resource, and the question is how to manage it. The ELM adds a prior and more uncomfortable layer: the parties to this conflict do not disagree about the forest. They inhabit different forests. They enact different worlds. And those worlds are now in pathological coupling — one organised to extract, one organised to relate, and a third caught between them, carrying regulatory overhead it cannot sustain. This is not a resource management failure. It is a world-collision — and the Four Rooms of each world have been running in incompatible coupling configurations for over a century.
On Method, Evidence, and the Pluriverse Frame

This analysis applies the Existential Load Model to a multi-world collision using the pluriverse framework developed through the work of Arturo Escobar and the relational ontology literature, mapped onto the dynamical systems architecture of the ELM. Three world-systems are analysed: the industrial forestry world, the First Nations world, and the provincial state world that mediates between them. Factual substrate draws on the 2020 Old-Growth Strategic Review (Merkel & Gorley), BC government old-growth data current to February 2025, reporting by The Narwhal and the Ancient Forest Alliance, and Sierra Club BC's November 2025 analysis. Nation-specific context draws on publicly documented positions of the Pacheedaht, Ahousaht, Tla-o-qui-aht, Hupačasath, Tseshaht, and Yuułuʔiłʔatḥ Nations, among others.

The ELM mapping is interpretive. It applies a theoretical lens to documented facts. The analysis does not adjudicate the political or legal dispute. It maps the dynamical structure of why the conflict persists, what each world's load signature looks like from inside, and what coupling configuration would be required for genuine resolution rather than managed continuation.

These numbers describe a system under acute constraint — ecological, political, and cultural — that has been managed through a sustained pattern the Wilderness Committee terms "talk-and-log": genuine consultation processes running in parallel with continued extraction, such that the consultation absorbs political pressure while the logging absorbs the forest. The 2020 Old-Growth Strategic Review called for an immediate paradigm shift. Five years later, the province has simultaneously implemented partial deferrals and instructed a 50 percent logging increase. This is not inconsistency. Through the ELM, it is the signature of a system caught between two incompatible coupling architectures, unable to let go of either, sustaining both at enormous regulatory cost.

Part One
The Three Worlds

The pluriverse concept distinguishes between having different perspectives on the same world and actually inhabiting different worlds — worlds enacted through different practices, different relationships, and different ontological commitments. This distinction matters acutely here: the conflict cannot be resolved by better communication between parties who share a world. It requires recognising that these parties do not share a world, and that any durable resolution must create conditions for different worlds to maintain stable phase relationships without one annihilating the other.

THE EXTRACTIVE WORLD
Industrial forestry — fibre, tenure, economic continuity
AGENCY / PREDICTION
Prediction Room: Oriented to fibre supply, annual allowable cut, quarterly results, employment statistics. The forest's future is a yield curve. "Sustainability" is defined as continued harvestable supply, not ecosystem persistence.

Agency Room: Vast institutional capacity — tenure rights, capital, machinery, political relationships. Action means permits, roads, falling, and milling. The agency loop is strongly coupled to economic output and deeply entrained to short-cycle rhythms of fiscal quarters and political terms.

Identity Room: "We build communities. We put food on tables. We are the backbone of rural BC." The self-model is constituted by productive transformation — turning standing timber into board-feet, jobs, and tax revenue. A tree left standing is a failure of agency.

Meaning Room: The progress-and-development narrative. Resource extraction as civilisational service. The forest exists to be used. Leaving it intact is sentimental, economically illiterate, or politically captured. A leaked technical review in 2025 found the province's timber harvest is vastly overestimated — the Meaning Room narrative of "sustainable yield" is no longer supported by the underlying ecology, but the narrative precedes the evidence.
SYSTEM LOAD — CONCEALED, RISING
THE FIRST NATIONS WORLD
Custodial relationship — place, kinship, intergenerational continuity
MEANING / IDENTITY
Prediction Room: Oriented to long cyclical time — seasonal rounds, intergenerational stewardship, reciprocal obligation to the land that has held for millennia. The forest's future is the continuation of a living relationship. A clearcut does not disrupt a supply chain — it severs a kinship network.

Agency Room: Custodial and ceremonial. Action means maintaining the practices that keep the relationship alive — harvesting medicines, performing ceremonies, exercising stewardship, speaking the language that carries the world's meaning. The Wilderness Committee notes that for many Nations, these forests are simultaneously home, medicine cabinets, storybooks, and ancestors — not metaphorically, but literally.

Identity Room: Place-constituted. Identity is not portable. The Pacheedaht, the Ahousaht, the Tla-o-qui-aht — these identities are not held inside individual bodies that happen to live in a particular location. They are enacted through specific relationships with specific places. When the place is destroyed, identity does not move to a new location. It fragments.

Meaning Room: Cosmological integration across all scales. Harm to land is harm to self is harm to ancestors is harm to future generations. The meaning loop does not separate ecological from cultural from spiritual harm. It cannot be partially satisfied by economic compensation, because the harm being named is not economic.
SYSTEM LOAD — TRANSMITTED, CHRONIC, ACUTE
THE PROVINCIAL WORLD
State mediation — between worlds it cannot fully see
PREDICTION / IDENTITY
Prediction Room: Caught between incompatible futures. The forestry sector's near-term revenue model and the ecological reality of accelerating old-growth depletion generate irreconcilable Prediction Room signals. The government's 2025 instruction to raise logging levels by 50% while maintaining paradigm-shift commitments is not hypocrisy — it is the behavioural signature of a Prediction Room that cannot integrate contradictory models.

Agency Room: Formally large — regulatory, legislative, financial — but functionally constrained by tenure law, constitutional obligations to First Nations, electoral cycles, and an economy in which forestry-dependent communities face genuine transition costs. The agency loop is oscillating between genuine commitment and structural inability to act on it.

Identity Room: The provincial self-model as "responsible steward" has fractured. The 2020 OGSR promised a paradigm shift. Five years of talk-and-log have made the gap between the declared identity and the actual coupling architecture visible to all parties. This is an Identity Room under severe strain — the self-model requires protection while the actions it produces contradict it.

Meaning Room: The province attempts to hold a meaning architecture that simultaneously validates Indigenous rights, ecological science, economic development, and democratic accountability. These are not compatible frameworks. The regulatory overhead of maintaining their simultaneous coherence is the defining load of provincial governance in this domain.
SYSTEM LOAD — STRUCTURAL, PARALYTIC
Part Two
The Four Rooms at System Scale

When the ELM is applied to a multi-world system, the Four Rooms operate at the level of the collision itself — not just within each world. The system-level rooms describe the state of the coupling architecture between worlds, not the internal state of any single actor.

01
PREDICTION ROOM
Incommensurable Futures Running in Parallel
EXTRACTIVEFIRST NATIONSPROVINCIAL
The system's Prediction Room is fragmented into three irreconcilable future models. The extractive world predicts a supply-managed transition to second-growth if adequately resourced. The First Nations world predicts continued erasure unless custodial authority is legally transferred. The provincial world predicts both futures simultaneously and can act on neither. No shared model of what the forest will be in 50 years exists across these worlds. Policy debates proceed as if they are disagreements within a shared Prediction Room — which is why they produce process without resolution. The prediction divergence is ontological, not empirical.
02
AGENCY ROOM
Asymmetric Capacity, Severed Feedback
EXTRACTIVEFIRST NATIONS
The agency asymmetry is stark. The industrial world holds tenure rights backed by provincial law, capital equipment, and road access. The First Nations world holds custodial relationships and a constitutional right to consultation that has been structurally deployed to absorb conflict rather than transfer authority. The UBCIC and BC Assembly of First Nations described the deferral process as a "hot potato" — passing difficult decisions to Nations without adequate transition funding or genuine decision-making power. High industrial agency is coupled to severed feedback: vast capacity for action, systematically insulated from the full consequences of that action. The result is agency without coherence — a loop that can do enormous work while systematically mis-predicting its own effects.
03
IDENTITY ROOM
Three Self-Models, Each Constituted by the Others' Absence
EXTRACTIVEFIRST NATIONSPROVINCIAL
The three identity architectures are mutually constituted through negation. The extractive world's identity as "productive" requires the dismissal of the custodial world as "sentimental." The First Nations' identity as sovereign requires the recognition that the province has structurally refused to fully grant. The province's identity as "reconciliation leader" requires outcomes its own institutional structure prevents it from producing. Each world's self-model is partially stabilised by the other worlds' failure to achieve what they claim. This is the deepest coupling pathology in the system — identity architectures that are energetically dependent on the conflict continuing, even when all parties genuinely want resolution.
04
MEANING ROOM
Cosmology vs. Economics — With No Common Language
EXTRACTIVEFIRST NATIONSPROVINCIAL
The meaning frameworks are not in disagreement. They are in different languages. The industrial Meaning Room speaks board-feet, jobs, GDP contribution, and carbon sequestration credits. The First Nations Meaning Room speaks reciprocal obligation, ancestral presence, cultural continuity, and the non-negotiable aliveness of what the industrial world categorises as "timber." The provincial Meaning Room attempts translation and produces documents that satisfy neither world's meaning architecture. Grand Chief Stewart Phillip named this precisely: the failure to deliver on old-growth commitments is "a profound betrayal of trust in First Nations, the public and future generations." Betrayal is a Meaning Room wound — it names the rupture of an obligation that was understood as sacred, not contractual.
Part Three
The Coupling Architecture

The ELM identifies three failure modes of coupling between oscillating systems: runaway coupling (escalating positive feedback), rigid coupling (locked patterns at high energy cost), and fragmented coupling (decoupling, loss of coordination). All three are present in the BC old-growth system — operating across different loops and different world-pairs simultaneously.

Coupling Mode World Pair Loop What It Looks Like
Runaway coupling Extractive ↔ Extractive Agency + Prediction Self-amplifying extraction logic: more roads justify more harvest justify more infrastructure investment justify more roads. The internal coupling of the industrial world's Agency and Prediction loops has positive feedback that operates independently of external signals. The 2025 instruction to raise logging levels came as the underlying fibre supply was collapsing — Prediction Room amplification defeating its own factual inputs.
Rigid coupling Provincial ↔ Extractive Identity + Meaning Tenure law as locked coupling architecture: the province's identity as a legitimate government is constituted by the enforcement of tenure rights it granted to the industrial world. Unlocking this requires identity-level change, not policy adjustment. The BC NDP's "paradigm shift" language names the identity shift required without producing it — the gap between declaration and action is the signature of an Identity Room that cannot afford to fully update.
Fragmented coupling First Nations ↔ System Agency + Meaning Custodial practice severed from custodial territory: Nations whose identity is place-constituted and whose agency is custodial have been coupled to a system that has progressively destroyed the places that make that identity and agency coherent. The "hot potato" deferral process is fragmented coupling institutionalised — nominal connection (consultation) without the actual coupling required for meaningful coordination (decision-making authority).
Partial coherence Ahousaht + Tla-o-qui-aht ↔ Province All rooms The 2024 conservancy agreements as proof of concept: In June 2024, in partnership with Ahousaht and Tla-o-qui-aht First Nations, 10 new conservancies were established covering approximately 76,000 hectares. This is the ELM's phase coherence model in practice — not merger, not dominance, but a stable coupling arrangement that preserves each world's internal coherence while creating a shared regulatory architecture. The energy cost is sustainable because both worlds' meaning loops are served by the outcome.
The system's signature pathology is not that worlds disagree. It is that the dominant world's agency loop has been structurally insulated from the Meaning Room of the world it is destroying — and the provincial mediating world carries the regulatory overhead of maintaining that insulation while simultaneously claiming to be dismantling it.
Core ELM Finding
The Distributed Load of World-Suppression

The ELM's most important contribution to this analysis is its account of where the load goes when one world suppresses another. Standard analysis tracks the ecological cost (habitat loss, species collapse), the economic cost (transition funding needed, industry jobs at risk), and the political cost (legal challenges, civil disobedience, reputational damage to the province). These are real. But the ELM tracks a prior cost that generates all of them: the energetic cost of maintaining a coupling architecture that systematically prevents one world from enacting itself.

The load does not disappear when one world dominates another. It distributes through the coupling network. The First Nations world carries it as chronic existential load — the Prediction, Agency, Identity, and Meaning rooms all running under the persistent perturbation of a dominant system that is destroying the relational field through which those rooms are enacted. This load is intergenerational. It accumulates. It propagates through families and communities as exactly the symptoms the ELM maps: collapsed agency, fragmented identity, meaning-room crisis, prediction-room failure.

The extractive world carries a different but real load: the cognitive cost of maintaining a meaning architecture that is increasingly contradicted by evidence. The leaked 2025 technical review finding that BC's timber harvest estimates are vastly overestimated is a Prediction Room signal that the system's meaning architecture is actively suppressing. The energy cost of that suppression — the regulatory, political, and narrative work required to maintain the One-World-World's claim to universal validity against accumulating counter-evidence — is not zero. It is a structural tax on every actor in the system.

The provincial world carries the most visible load: the regulatory overhead of mediating between two worlds while pretending to inhabit one. Every consultation process, every deferral negotiation, every reconciliation commitment is an attempt to create partial connections between worlds that the province's own institutional architecture is designed to keep in asymmetric relation. The "talk-and-log" pattern is not a political failure. It is the predictable output of a system that cannot let go of the coupling architecture it is simultaneously claiming to transform.

Diagnosis
What the System Cannot See
INCOHERENCE PATTERN
The Consultation Loop as
Gain-Adding Mechanism
The province's consultation process is formally designed to reduce conflict. The ELM reveals its structural effect: it adds gain to the conflict's Identity Room loops. Every consultation that produces no change in practice confirms the First Nations world's model that consultation is designed to absorb rather than transfer authority — amplifying the identity signal that the system is not operating in good faith. Every consultation that produces partial change confirms the extractive world's model that pressure works incrementally — amplifying its agency loop's resistance to structural change. The process designed to reduce load is generating it. This is not a design flaw. It is the inevitable output of a consultation architecture that sits inside the existing coupling pattern rather than changing it.
ROOT LOAD
Ontological Asymmetry
Without Acknowledgement
The generative condition is the province's institutional refusal to acknowledge that it is adjudicating between worlds rather than managing competing interests within one. When a court grants legal standing to a development permit, it is not making a neutral procedural decision — it is enacting the world in which the forest is a resource with legal tenure attached, and not the world in which the forest is a living ancestor with sovereign custodians. The ontological asymmetry is embedded in the legal infrastructure itself. This is what the ELM, following the pluriverse literature, calls worldcide-by-architecture: the destruction of other worlds not through explicit suppression but through the routine operation of a legal and economic system that only recognises one world as real.
THE DECISIVE ASYMMETRY
Irreversibility as
Runaway Attractor
Less than three per cent of BC's most productive old-growth remains. The system has a dominant attractor: once logged, the old-growth world cannot be re-entered. This irreversibility creates asymmetric stakes that the ELM maps precisely. The extractive world can change its practices and survive. The industrial world can transition to second-growth and persist. The provincial state can update its legal architecture and continue governing. The First Nations world — whose identity, meaning, and agency are constituted through specific ancient trees, specific valleys, specific relationships with non-human persons that are several centuries old — cannot be restored after the last three per cent is gone. The asymmetry of reversibility is the asymmetry of the stakes. No negotiation framework that ignores this produces durable outcomes.
THE STABILISATION PROBLEM
No Coherent Oscillator
at the Required Scale
The ELM identifies stabilisation as requiring a coherent external oscillator — a system stable enough to provide regulating entrainment to a dysregulated field. In the BC old-growth system, the institution that would theoretically serve this function — the provincial government — is itself inside the pathological coupling architecture, carrying its own load, structurally unable to hold steady. The partial successes — the 2024 Ahousaht and Tla-o-qui-aht conservancy agreements, specific deferral areas where First Nations have genuine authority — share a common structural feature: they transfer actual regulatory capacity to the world with the deepest attractor basin. The First Nations world, precisely because its meaning loop integrates ecological, cultural, and spiritual care into a single coherent framework, is the more stable oscillator. Resolution follows from recognising this, not managing against it.
Synthesis
What the Model Reveals
01
THE CORE FINDING
The conflict is stable because the coupling architecture is more stable than any policy intervention operating within it.
Tenure law, consultation processes, deferral mechanisms — these are all interventions within the existing coupling architecture. They change the parameters of a system that remains structurally unchanged. The conflict will persist until the architecture itself changes — which requires the province to recognise that it has been a world-suppression mechanism, not a neutral mediator, and to design institutions that can hold multiple worlds rather than adjudicating between them within the terms of one.
02
THE COUNTERINTUITIVE FINDING
The extractive world is also carrying load it cannot name — and naming it is required for resolution.
The industrial forestry world's workers, community members, and managers are not carrying no load. They carry the load of a Meaning Room architecture that is increasingly contradicted by evidence — a narrative of sustainable yield that the underlying ecology no longer supports. Resolution is not available through defeating the extractive world. It requires creating a meaning architecture generous enough to hold its legitimate grief — the real loss of livelihoods, communities, and identities that a genuine paradigm shift entails. No resolution framework that ignores this will produce durable outcomes.
03
THE ELM FINDING
RALG at civilisational scale: what Recognize, Accept, Let Go requires of the dominant world.
Recognize: the coupling exists. The exhaustion, the legal battles, the ecological collapse — these are not external interference. They are feedback from a world being destroyed through the coupling patterns of the dominant system. Accept: stop adding gain. The legal frameworks, economic models, and identity narratives that require the One-World-World's continued claim to universality are gain added to loops already amplified past sustainability. Let Go: release the regulatory overhead of the One-World-World. The energy cost of suppressing other worlds is measurable. Letting go does not leave nothing — it frees the energy currently consumed in that suppression for actual coordination.
04
THE POLITICAL FINDING
Phase coherence, not policy reform, is the structural requirement.
The 2024 Ahousaht and Tla-o-qui-aht conservancy agreements are the ELM's proof-of-concept for what resolution looks like: not merger of worlds, not one world defeating the other, but a stable phase relationship in which each world's internal coherence is maintained and their points of contact are governed by genuinely shared architecture. The operative variable is not the number of protected hectares. It is whether the coupling architecture allows each world to maintain its own regulatory loops. When it does, the energy cost becomes sustainable. When it doesn't, no amount of policy refinement resolves the structural load.
05
THE PREDICTIVE CLAIM
The system will continue in talk-and-log unless the Province releases its identity-level investment in being the mediating world.
The ELM generates a falsifiable prediction: as long as the province's identity loop requires it to be the arbiter between worlds — rather than recognising that it has been a participant in world-suppression — it will produce consultation without authority transfer, deferral without protection, and paradigm-shift language without paradigm-shift architecture. The operative room is the provincial Identity Room. It is the room from which genuine change would have to begin. No forestry policy reform that does not address this room will produce the outcome it nominally seeks.
06
MODEL DEVELOPMENT NOTE
This analysis extends the ELM into pluriverse territory for the first time.
Prior ELM analyses identified load carried by individual actors or within single organisational systems. This case reveals a structurally different condition: load generated and distributed across a multi-world collision, where the coupling architecture itself is the mechanism of harm. The concept of "transmitted load" — existential load generated in one world and carried in another through pathological coupling — extends the ELM's conceptual vocabulary. So does the concept of "worldcide-by-architecture": the destruction of a world not through explicit violence but through the routine operation of a legal and economic system that recognises only one world as real.

The BC old-growth conflict is not best understood as a failure of policy, political will, or communication between parties who share a world. It is a world-collision in which three incompatible coupling architectures have been held in contact for over a century — one organised to extract, one organised to relate, and one caught between them, carrying the regulatory overhead of pretending to mediate what it has structurally been designed to perpetuate. Understanding this does not dissolve the moral and political stakes. It locates them precisely — in the coupling architecture itself, in the legal and institutional infrastructure that recognises only one world as real, and in the Identity Rooms that would have to change before any other room can. Less than three per cent of the most productive old-growth remains. The attractor is almost irreversible. The model is not the forest. But the forest is running out of time for the model to be ignored.
